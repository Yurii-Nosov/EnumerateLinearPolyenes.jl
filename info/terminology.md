# Terminology

## Chemical  terminology

All standard chemical terminology can be found on the [IUPAC](https://goldbook.iupac.org/) website.

### Isomers

In chemistry, isomers are molecules or polyatomic ions with the same molecular formula, that is, with the same number of atoms of each element, but with a different arrangement of atoms in space.

Despite having the same atomic composition, isomers can have different physical properties such as boiling point, melting point, and chemical reactivity.

There are several types of isomers, for example:

Structural isomers (or constitutional isomers) are distinguished by bonds between atoms. Example: dimethyl ether and ethanol (C2H6O).

Stereoisomers (or spatial isomers) have the same bonds, but different relative positions of atoms.

Geometric isomers (such as cis and trans) occur when a molecule has a rigid structure that prohibits free rotation around a bond.

Additional information on [isomers](https://www.masterorganicchemistry.com/2018/09/10/types-of-isomers/).

### Conformers in chemistry

Conformers (rotary isomers) are stereoisomers corresponding to different potential energy minima of a molecule. The term comes from the concept of "conformation" (from Latin conformatio — "form, structure, location").

Basic definition
The conformation of a molecule is the spatial arrangement of atoms in a molecule of a certain configuration, resulting from:

turning around single sigma bonds;

in some cases, pyramidal inversions and polytopic rearrangements (for inorganic and organoelement compounds).

A conformer is a specific spatial shape of a molecule corresponding to one of the minima of potential energy.

Important features Conformers are not isomers in the classical sense: they are formed without breaking chemical bonds, by rotating around bonds. It is almost impossible to distinguish a separate conformer due to the rapid transition between conformations.

Additional information on [conformers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotamer).

### Molecular configuration

The molecular configuration molecule is the permanent geometry that results from the spatial arrangement of its bonds.[1] The ability of the same set of atoms to form two or more molecules with different configurations is stereoisomerism. This is distinct from constitutional isomerism which arises from atoms being connected in a different order. Conformers which arise from single bond rotations, if not isolatable as atropisomers, do not count as distinct molecular configurations as the spatial connectivity of bonds is identical.

Additional information on [configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_configuration).

### Molecular conformations

The conformation of a molecule (from Latin conformatio, meaning “form, structure, arrangement”) is the spatial arrangement of atoms within a molecule of a certain configuration, determined by rotation around one or more single sigma bonds. In some cases, conformational transformations also include pyramidal inversions and other polytopal rearrangements of inorganic and organometallic compounds.

Stereoisomers in conformations corresponding to the minima of potential energy are called conformers [(rotational isomers)](https://web.archive.org/web/20101221034749/http://goldbook.iupac.org/C01258.html).

### Unbranched conjugated polyene hydrocarbons

Unbranched conjugated hydrocarbons are organic compounds with a linear, non-branching carbon backbone containing alternating single and double bonds (-electron systems). Examples include linear polyenes
(e.g., 1,3,5-hexatriene) and unbranched catacondensed benzenoid hydrocarbons (e.g., anthracene, naphthalene). These systems, characterized by significant $\pi$-electron delocalization, show increased stability with chain length.
  
**Key Aspects of Unbranched Conjugated Hydrocarbons:**

- **Structure:** Composed of carbon and hydrogen only, these molecules feature a straight or zig-zagging chain (or fused ring system) where every alternate carbon-carbon bond is a double bond, allowing for resonance (delocalization).

- **Types:**
Acyclic Conjugated Polyenes: Linear chains like (1,3,5-hexatriene).
Unbranched Catacondensed Benzenoids: Fused aromatic rings in a linear or angled chain, such as anthracene or phenanthrene, where no carbon belongs to more than two rings.

### Molecular graph

In chemical graph theory and in mathematical chemistry molecular graph or chemical graph is a representation of the structural formula chemical compound in terms of graph theory.

A chemical graph is a labeled graph whose vertices correspond to the atoms of the compound and edges correspond to chemical bonds. Its vertices are labeled with the kinds of the corresponding atoms and edges are labeled with the types of bonds. For particular purposes any of the labelings may be ignored.

A hydrogen-depleted molecular graph or hydrogen-suppressed molecular graph is the molecular graph with hydrogen vertices deleted.
In some important cases (topological index calculation etc.) the following classical definition is sufficient: a molecular graph is a connected, undirected graph which admits a one-to-one correspondence with the structural formula of a chemical compound in which the vertices of the graph correspond to atoms of the molecule and edges of the graph correspond to chemical bonds between these atoms.

## Mathematical terminology

### Graph

The fundamental object of study in graph theory, a system of vertices connected in pairs by edges. Often subdivided into directed graphs or undirected graphs according to whether the edges have an orientation or not. Mixed graphs include both types of edges
A simple graph is a graph without loops and without multiple adjacencies. That is, each edge connects two distinct endpoints and no two edges have the same endpoints. A simple edge is an edge that is not part of a multiple adjacency. In many cases, graphs are assumed to be simple unless specified otherwise.

### Order

The order of a graph G is the number of its vertices, |V(G)|. The variable n is often used for this quantity. See also size, the number of edges.

A lower-case n is often used (especially in computer science) to denote the number of vertices in a given graph.

### Size

The size of a graph G is the number of its edges, |E(G)|. The variable m is often used for this quantity. See also order, the number of vertices

### Degree

The degree of a vertex in a graph is its number of incident edges. The degree of a graph G (or its maximum degree) is the maximum of the degrees of its vertices, often denoted Δ(G); the minimum degree of G is the minimum of its vertex degrees, often denoted δ(G). Degree is sometimes called valency; the degree of v in G may be denoted dG(v), d(G), or deg(v).

### Leaf

A leaf vertex or pendant vertex (especially in a tree) is a vertex whose degree is 1.
A leaf edge or pendant edge is the edge connecting a leaf vertex to its single neighbour.

### Walk

A walk is a finite or infinite sequence of edges which joins a sequence of vertices. Walks are also sometimes called chains.  A walk is open if its first and last vertices are distinct, and closed if they are repeated.

### Trail

A walk without repeated edges.

### Simple path

 A simple path or a simple cycle is a path or cycle that has no repeated vertices and consequently no repeated edges.

### Distance

The distance between any two vertices in a graph is the length of the shortest path having the two vertices as its endpoints.

### Path graph

A path graph is a graph with at least two vertices is connected and has two terminal vertices (vertices of degree 1), while all others (if any) have degree 2.  

### Tree

 A tree is an undirected graph that is both connected and acyclic, or a directed graph in which there exists a unique walk from one vertex (the root of the tree) to all remaining vertices.

### Caterpillar or caterpillar tree

In graph theory, a caterpillar or caterpillar tree is a tree in which all the vertices are within distance 1 of a central path.

### Internal  vertex of a path graph

A vertex of a path graph $P_n$ is internal if it is not a leaf; that is, if its degree is greater than one. and an internal edge of $P_n$ is an edge that connects internal vertices.

### canonical representative method

The canonical representative method described in [Brinkmann G.][5] is defined as follows:

- A specific encoding method for graphs is selected;
- A criterion for choosing a canonical code for isomorphic graphs is defined (usually, either the smallest or largest code is chosen as the canonical code);
- All possible graphs are created along with their codes, and graphs with non-canonical codes are rejected.

## Terminology introduced in the article

### Edge Configuration

The configuration of an internal edge of a molecular graph is a spatial arrangement of two edges adjacent to this edge. Each internal edge of a molecular graph can be in one of two configurations: trans or cis.

### Trans Configuration

The trans configuration of an internal edge $e_i$ is a spatial arrangement of its adjacent edges $e_{i-1}$ and $e_{i+1}$ such that they are on opposite sides of a plane passing through the edge $e_i$. We say that such an edge has a trans configuration or is in a trans configuration.

### Cis Configuration

The cis configuration of an internal edge $e_i$ is a spatial arrangement of its adjacent edges $e_{i-1}$ and $e_{i+1}$ such that they are on the same side of a plane passing through the edge $e_i$. We say that such an edge has a cis-configuration or is in a cis-configuration.

### Cisoid path

A cisoid path is a simple path with two or more consecutive edges with a cis-configuration. The length of a cisoid path is the number of its edges in a cis-configuration.
A cisoid path with k or more edges is denoted by $Cis^+_k$
A cisoid path with k edges is denoted by $Cis_k$.

### Reversal and Inversion Operations

For a binary tuple $B = (b_1, b_2, . . . , b_k)$, the reversal operation
Rev(B) is the reverse permutation of its elements, that is,
$Rev(B) = (b_k, b_{k−1}, . . . , b_2, b_1)$.
Similarly, the inversion operation Inv(B) is the replacement of all zeros
with ones and all ones with zeros, that is,
$Inv(B) = (\overline b_1,\overline b_2,\dots,\overline b_k)$,
where $\overline b_i$ is the inverted element of $b_i$.

### Symmetric and IR-symmetric tuples

Tuples B for which B = Rev(B) are called symmetric tuples, and tuples B for which
B = Inv(Rev(B)) or B = Rev(Inv(B)) are called IR-symmetric tuples.

### Order of a Molecular Graph

The order of a molecular graph with hydrogen atoms of a linear polyene is the number of vertices in its central chain. This value coincides with the order of a molecular graph with suppressed hydrogen atoms. In our programs, the order of a molecular graph is specified by the variable `n`, or `order`.

### class

A class of graphs or family of graphs is a (usually infinite) collection of graphs, often defined as the graphs having some specific property. The word "class" is used rather than "set" because, unless special restrictions are made (such as restricting the vertices to be drawn from a particular set, and defining edges to be sets of two vertices) classes of graphs are usually not sets when formalized using set theory.

### Classes of molecular graphs of linear polyenes

Molecular graphs of linear polyenes are divided into three classes:

- isomers;
- conformers of trans-isomers;
- conformers of other isomers.

#### Properties of isomers, conformers of trans-isomers and conformers of other isomers

- In trans isomers, all double bonds form trans configurations, and all single bonds form trans conformations.
- In other isomers, double bonds form trans configurations, form both trans and cis configurations, and all single bonds form trans configurations.
- In the conformers of trans isomers, all double bonds form trans configurations, and single bonds form both trans and cis conformations.
- In the conformers of other isomers, double bonds form both trans and cis configurations, and single bonds form both trans and cis conformations.

Molecular graphs of conformers of other isomers are divided into subclasses:

- graphs containing cisoid chains of length 4 or longer;
- graphs not containing cisoid chains of length 4 or longer;
- graphs containing cisoid chains of length 3;
- graphs not containing cisoid chains of length 3; such graphs were called Yeh graphs
  in the article by Yu. L. Nosov and M. N. Nazarov.
